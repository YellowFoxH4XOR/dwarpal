# Dwarpal: code review and novelty assessment against the literature

Read against the code (commit from `main`, July 2026): `internal/engine`, the nine gates under `internal/gates/`, `internal/repoindex`, `internal/gitio`, the PRD, and the V1 checklist (73/84 done). This is not a concept — it is a working, deterministic-first, agent-agnostic pre-commit quality firewall in Go. The assessment below is in two halves: where your design has *already* independently rediscovered the field's hard-won lessons (a strength — it means the architecture is sound), and where the genuinely unoccupied ground still is.

## The pre-commit reframing is the real novelty

Your PRD's category claim — "first open-source, agent-agnostic **pre-commit** quality firewall for AI-authored code" — is the strongest thing here, and it survives contact with the literature. The academic and commercial work I surveyed sits almost entirely *post-hoc*. [CodeAgent (Zhang et al. 2024)](https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2402.02172), [RovoDev at Atlassian (2026)](https://doi.org/10.1145/3786583.3786851), and CodeRabbit/Greptile/Qodo all review a PR *after* the diff exists. Your insight that the leverage moved to the git boundary *before* the commit lands is correct and under-served. The one paper that shares it, [AgenticSCR (2026)](https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2601.19138), confirms your thesis in its first sentence ("vulnerabilities must be caught early under tight latency and limited-context constraints") but scopes itself to *security* and to an *agentic* (LLM-driven) design. Your differentiation from it is real and defensible: agent-agnostic, deterministic-first, team-versioned config, git-boundary rather than security-only. Read AgenticSCR closely; it is your nearest neighbour in the research literature and a useful contrast in any writeup, not a threat.

## You re-derived the static-analysis playbook

The design discipline in `docs/why-harnesses-beat-prompts.md` and the fail-closed/fail-open split in `engine.go` and `intent.go` are, essentially, the Google static-analysis lessons rediscovered from first principles. [Sadowski et al. 2018, "Lessons from building static analysis tools at Google"](https://doi.org/10.1145/3188720) and [Tricorder (2015)](https://doi.org/10.1109/icse.2015.76) reached your exact conclusions: analyses must run *at review time* inside the workflow; the effective-false-positive rate is the single variable that decides whether developers keep the tool on (they found the threshold near one in ten); findings must be actionable with a suggested fix; and rule sets must be owned by the teams. Your `retry_hints`, the `p95 < 2s` budget, the `info`/`warn` severity floor for heuristic gates, and `dwarpal feedback` are each a direct instantiation of one of those lessons. This is a strength worth making explicit: citing Sadowski moves your README's design choices from "founder's taste" to "the documented consensus of the largest static-analysis deployment in the industry." It also arms you against risk R5 ("yet another linter"): you are not another linter, you are Tricorder's playbook applied at the pre-commit boundary for agent code.

Similarly, your `convention_drift` gate is a lightweight, deployed version of an idea with a real research pedigree. [Pradel & Sen 2018 (DeepBugs)](https://doi.org/10.1145/3276517) framed a whole bug class as *deviation from learned naming/usage conventions*, precisely what `drift.go` does when it flags a `snake_case` function in a camelCase repo. The naming-distribution fingerprint in `repoindex` is the intuition Allamanis's convention-mining line formalized. You are on solid ground here; the gate is honest about being heuristic (`severity: info`), which is exactly right.

## Code-level findings

**1. The intent gate sees only added lines — it structurally cannot detect the scope violations it promises.** In `internal/gates/intent/intent.go`, `buildPrompt` iterates `f.AddedLines` only. A diff that *deletes* an auth check, removes a guard clause, or strips a test is invisible to the very gate whose job is "does this do *only* what was asked?" The most dangerous agent edits, silent removals, pass clean. Feed removed lines and a hunk of surrounding context, not just additions. This is the single highest-value code fix in the review.

**2. The intent prompt is zero-shot free-text against a structured `Verdict`, fragile exactly where the field says it breaks.** `Verdict{AccomplishesIntent, OnlyStatedIntent, Surprises}` is parsed out of an open-ended model response. [CR-Bench (2026)](https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2603.11078) reports the field's central failure mode bluntly: LLM reviewers tuned to catch every issue produce a low signal-to-noise ratio that erodes trust, the modern restatement of Google's false-positive lesson. Two mitigations, both cheap: (a) use provider tool-use / structured-output to force the `Verdict` schema instead of parsing prose, and (b) give each judgment a confidence and let the gate *abstain* ("no confident violation") rather than always emitting a warn. [CodeAgent's](https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2402.02172) supervisory "QA-Checker" agent exists for exactly this reason; you can get 80% of that value with a confidence threshold and no second model call.

**3. Rules are documented and configurable but not *example-tested*, and this is your biggest missed opportunity.** You have 20+ rules under `docs/rules/`, per-rule `disable`, and `dwarpal feedback` for false-positive reports — but no rule carries a fixture set of positive/negative example diffs, and there is no per-rule precision number. This is the gap my earlier survey identified as unoccupied across the *entire* literature, and it maps onto your architecture almost for free: a `testdata/rules/<rule-id>/{should_flag,should_pass}/*.diff` tree, run in CI, turns every rule into a regression-tested artifact and lets you publish a per-rule precision table. That table is what converts Dwarpal from "a tool with good defaults" into "a tool with *measured* defaults," and directly attacks risk R4 (false positives erode trust) with data instead of a severity default.

**4. Language coverage is the practical ceiling.** Naming and error-idiom drift are Go-only; import-style spans the tree-sitter languages; the AST rules cover Go/TS/JS/Python. That is a reasonable v1 cut, but for a tool whose thesis is "agent code in any repo," the Go-centricity of the smartest gates will show through. The M4 plan (Rust/Java grammar) is the right instinct; I'd prioritize whichever language your first design partners actually write.

## Where a genuine research contribution is hiding

You do not need a paper to ship this, but if you want the project to have scientific standing (useful for enterprise credibility and for the "not yet another linter" fight), there is one clear, defensible contribution nobody has published:

**A rule-conformance benchmark.** [CR-Bench](https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2603.11078) and [c-CRAB (2026)](https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2603.23448) both appeared in 2026 and both evaluate *general* review quality. Neither answers "given this rule set and these commits, did the system flag the right deviations on the right commits?" You are positioned to build that dataset because your fixture tree (finding 3) *is* the seed of it: a corpus of `(rule set, diff, expected findings)` triples, scored on precision-at-flag and per-rule recall, is a benchmark contribution of the same shape as CR-Bench but aimed at conformance rather than open-ended review. Ship it alongside the tool and it doubles as your own regression suite.

The vision paper [Rethinking Code Review in the Age of AI (2026)](https://doi.org/10.48550/arXiv.2605.17548) makes the timing argument you should borrow for your launch narrative: AI code generation is inflating review volume faster than review capacity, and current tooling is fragmented across isolated sub-tasks. Dwarpal's answer, one deterministic gate at the boundary every change must cross, is a clean response to a problem the literature is actively naming right now.

## Bottom line

The architecture is sound and, in several places, ahead of the published work on *deployment discipline*: retry_hints as a first-class agent-loop output, provenance-based `agent-only` gating, and the fail-closed/fail-open contract. The reframing to pre-commit is under-served and defensible against everything except AgenticSCR, from which you differ cleanly. Three changes would make the strongest version of the project: fix the added-lines-only blind spot in the intent gate (correctness), add per-rule example fixtures with a published precision table (trust, and the seed of a benchmark), and give the LLM gate structured output with calibrated abstention (signal-to-noise). The first is a bug fix; the second and third are the difference between a good tool and a citable one.
